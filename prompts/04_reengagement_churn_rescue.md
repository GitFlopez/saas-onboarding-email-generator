# Prompt 4: Re-engagement & Churn Rescue Kit

## Purpose
Three separate email sequences for three distinct user states: at-risk (inactive but still subscribed), churned (recently cancelled), and win-back (churned 31-90 days ago). Each sequence ends with a "break-up email" — the counterintuitive final send that recovers 10-15% of otherwise lost accounts by respecting the user's decision.

## The Prompt

```
You are a SaaS retention specialist and email copywriter. Write a complete Re-engagement and Churn Rescue Kit for the following product:

**Product Name:** [PRODUCT NAME]
**ICP:** [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY SIZE] companies dealing with [PAIN POINT]
**Pricing:** [PRICE POINT]
**Primary churn reason (if known):** [e.g., "Users say they're too busy to implement", "Price objection after trial", "Needed a feature we don't have", "Never activated — product felt too complex"]
**Moment of value (aha moment):** [FROM PROMPT 1]
**Brand voice:** [formal / casual / playful / direct / expert]

---

Deliver 3 complete sequences:

---

## Sequence 1: At-Risk Re-engagement (7-14 Days Inactive, Still Subscribed)

Audience: Users who haven't logged in for 7-14 days but are still paying subscribers.
Goal: Get them back to the product and re-engaged before they churn voluntarily.

Write 4 emails:

**Email 1 — The Check-In** (Day 7 of inactivity)
- Subject line: [caring, not salesy — "I noticed you haven't been in lately"]
- Preview text:
- Body: [120-160 words] Acknowledge they've been busy. No guilt. Offer one specific reason to log back in that takes 5 minutes or less. End with a question.
- CTA: [direct product link to the most valuable daily action]

**Email 2 — The Value Reminder** (Day 10 of inactivity)
- Subject line: [benefit-forward — remind them what they're not getting]
- Preview text:
- Body: [100-140 words] What are they missing while they're away? Specific, concrete, ICP-relevant. Don't list features — describe a problem they're probably facing right now that your product solves.
- CTA:

**Email 3 — The Offer** (Day 12 of inactivity)
- Subject line: [offer framing]
- Preview text:
- Body: [100-130 words] A concession or added value: a free 1:1 setup call, a template, a feature unlock, an extended trial. Not a discount — at-risk users still have value in your product; a discount trains bad behavior.
- CTA:

**Email 4 — The Break-Up** (Day 14 of inactivity)
- Subject line: [honest — "Should I let you go?"]
- Preview text:
- Body: [80-120 words] Tell them you're pausing emails. Ask one question: what's not working? Provide a simple 2-option choice: re-engage or cancel cleanly. Be respectful. This email has a counterintuitively high reply rate.
- Primary CTA: ["Log back in" — one click]
- Secondary CTA: ["Cancel my account" — make it easy; removes resentment]

---

## Sequence 2: Churned User Sequence (Cancelled Within Last 30 Days)

Audience: Users who cancelled within the last 30 days.
Goal: Identify the real churn reason, fix their objection, and re-activate 10-20% within 30 days.

Write 4 emails (send 2, 5, 12, 25 days after cancellation):

**Email 1 — The Graceful Exit** (Day 2 post-cancellation)
- Subject line: [no pressure, just acknowledgment]
- Body: [80-120 words] Thank them. No hard sell. One question: "Would you be willing to tell me the main reason you cancelled?" Link to a 1-question survey or just say "hit reply." This is your market research.
- CTA: [Reply or survey link]

**Email 2 — The Objection Handler** (Day 5 post-cancellation)
- Subject line: [address the most common churn reason from your data]
- Body: [120-160 words] Address the #1 churn objection directly. If it's price: ROI reframe. If it's complexity: "most users have X set up in 15 minutes." If it's missing feature: "we shipped [X] last week." Be honest if you haven't solved it yet.
- CTA: [Reactivation link — one click to resume subscription]

**Email 3 — The Come-Back Offer** (Day 12 post-cancellation)
- Subject line: [specific offer, time-limited]
- Body: [100-130 words] A reactivation offer. First month at 50% off, or 2 weeks free, or a credit toward an annual plan. Frame it as a second chance, not desperation.
- CTA: [Claim offer — link with auto-applied promo code if possible]

**Email 4 — The Final Goodbye** (Day 25 post-cancellation)
- Subject line: ["Closing your account on [DATE]" — honesty wins]
- Body: [80-100 words] Tell them you're removing them from the list in 7 days. Final chance to come back. One direct question: "Is there anything we could have done differently?" This email generates more replies than any other — use them.
- CTA: [Reactivate | Unsubscribe — both visible]

---

## Sequence 3: Win-Back Campaign (Churned 31-90 Days Ago)

Audience: Users who churned more than 30 days ago but less than 90 days ago. Older than 90 days have very low recovery rates.
Goal: Re-engage the 5-10% who churned for situational reasons (budget cut, project ended, changed roles) and are now ready to return.

Write 4 emails (send at 45, 60, 75, 88 days post-cancellation):

**Email 1 — The "We've Changed" Email** (Day 45)
- Subject line: [time-based curiosity — "A lot has changed since you left"]
- Body: [120-160 words] Acknowledge time has passed. Lead with 2-3 specific improvements made since they left. Not a changelog — a story of what's different. Make it feel personal.
- CTA: [See what's new — link to product changelog or tour]

**Email 2 — The Social Proof Push** (Day 60)
- Subject line: [social proof + ICP-specific outcome]
- Body: [130-160 words] A short case study of someone in the exact same role who came back or who stayed and got a specific result. The "could have been you" email.
- CTA: [Restart for free — 2-week no-card reactivation trial]

**Email 3 — The Incentive Email** (Day 75)
- Subject line: [specific incentive, not generic "deal"]
- Body: [100-130 words] Time-limited incentive: annual plan at 3 months free, lifetime deal if applicable, or a premium feature unlock for returning subscribers. Make it feel exclusive.
- CTA: [Claim your returning member rate]

**Email 4 — The Final Break-Up** (Day 88 — 2 days before 90-day cutoff)
- Subject line: ["Last email from me (I mean it)" — low-pressure, honest]
- Body: [80-100 words] Acknowledge you've tried a few times. Respect their decision. Remove them from win-back emails. Leave the door open without being needy. End on a human note — not a corporate sign-off.
- Primary CTA: [Come back one more time]
- Secondary CTA: [Remove me from all marketing — honor it cleanly]
```

## Variables to Fill In
- `[PRIMARY CHURN REASON]` — if you don't know it, run a 1-question exit survey for 2 weeks before deploying this sequence. Email 2 of Sequence 2 depends on this.
- `[PRICE POINT]` — needed for the ROI reframe in Sequence 2 Email 2
- `[BRAND VOICE]` — the churn rescue sequences should be slightly warmer than your acquisition emails; human connection matters here

## Tips
- The break-up email (Email 4 of Sequence 1 and Email 4 of Sequences 2 and 3) typically generates a 20-40% reply rate. Build a workflow to handle those replies — either automated ("thanks for letting me know") or a real human response for high-value accounts.
- Never discount in Sequence 1 (at-risk). Discounting at-risk users teaches them to go inactive to get a deal. Reserve discounts for Sequence 2 (churned) and Sequence 3 (win-back).
- Win-back sequence timing matters: Day 45-90 is the sweet spot. Before Day 30, they've barely left. After Day 90, they've found another solution or forgotten your product exists.
- "Closing your account on [DATE]" subject lines consistently outperform vague subject lines in Sequence 2 Email 4. The specificity of a date triggers action.
